Housing Quality: Exploratory Analysis and Classification

Giacomo Saccaggi

The consumer-to-consumer (C2C) real estate market is a very complex field where subjective factors that are difficult to predict come into play. One of the variables that most influences how much a user is willing to pay for a property is the expert assessment. The objective of this study is to determine how it is possible to predict — and which factors influence — the experts' evaluation of building quality, so as to provide users interested in buying or selling a home with evaluation guidelines.

The research question is: how can building quality be assessed and what are the parameters that most influence the expert's rating?

To answer this question, approximately 20,000 records from 2014/2015 on house sales in King County (Washington State, USA) were analysed, evaluated through a measure called grade. During this project we will go step by step through all the analyses performed, initially trying to predict the grade variable and then evaluating the attributes that most influence it.

1. Introduction

The dataset contains 21,613 observations and 21 attributes ranging from the property price to its characteristics such as square footage or geographical location.

Figure 1: Attribute table and correlation matrix

On the kingcounty.gov website, the grade variable is described as a classification for construction quality that refers to the types of materials used and the quality of workmanship. Higher quality buildings (higher grade) cost more per unit to build and have a higher value.

The construction grade is determined by the assessor who evaluates the property for tax purposes, so it does not change unless a home is extensively renovated or remodelled. It is a very subjective task, yet it is based on detailed guidelines provided by the county. We will call grade the quality of a building.

Our first objective is to find a way to predict, based on house characteristics, what its grade is, so that we can identify anomalous data that may be erroneous. We would therefore like to implement a classifier through which we can identify such evaluations so that they can possibly be rechecked.

2. Preprocessing

2.1 Descriptive Analysis of the Dataset

First, we observe that there are no missing values. The sum of sqft_above and sqft_basement corresponds to sqft_living. We report below some significant plots: boxplots of price, bedrooms, bathrooms and sqft_living divided by grade, and scatterplots of these variables coloured by grade.

Figure 2: Boxplots and scatterplots by grade

The variable bathrooms is obtained as a sum: 1 = room with bath/tub, shower, WC, sink; 0.75 = shower, WC, sink; 0.5 = WC and sink; 0.25 = WC only. The variable floor may be non-integer when the building has a mezzanine or attic. If yr_renovated = 0, the house has never been renovated. Sqft_lot indicates the total square footage of the lot (house + garden). Waterfront can be 0 (no water view) or 1. View ranges from 0 to 4. Condition ranges from 1 to 5 describing the condition relative to the year built.

2.2 Data Cleaning

We used boxplots and scatterplots to identify anomalous data. We removed a property with 33 bedrooms but sqft_living similar to 2/3/4-bedroom homes (clearly erroneous), and another with sqft_living=13,540 but price=$2,280,000 — which turned out to be an equine veterinary clinic. We also removed properties with 0 bathrooms and 0 bedrooms that had incongruously high grade values.

Figure 3: Data cleaning — identifying the 33-bedroom outlier and zero-bathroom anomalies

After cleaning, the dataset went from 21,613 to 21,605 records.

3. Grade Prediction Model

3.1 Preliminary Observation

Through a Random Forest regression analysis, we found that price depends largely on grade. We therefore decided to remove price from our predictors, since our goal is to predict grade from objective house characteristics available before the sale.

3.2 Criteria for Subdividing Grade into Classes

We subdivided the grade variable into fewer classes based on confusion matrices, merging classes where the classifier made the most errors. We used the OrdinalClassClassifier (Frank & Hall), an algorithm specifically designed for ordinal multi-class variables. We compared 6 methods: NBTree, MultiClass, OrdinalClass, J48, NaiveBayes, and RandomForest.

Figure 4–7: Accuracy tables, Kappa coefficients, and confusion matrix (13 classes)

3.3 Results with 5 Classes

After merging classes based on the confusion matrix analysis, we arrived at a 5-class subdivision (A through E). Using Random Forest with 60 trees, we achieved:

  • Accuracy: 83.8%
  • Cohen's Kappa: 0.715

Figure 8–10: Confusion matrix with 5 classes, ROC curves, and performance comparison

4. Variable Importance

Using Random Forest (regression mode), we identified the most influential attributes for predicting grade. The top variables are: sqft_living, sqft_above, sqft_living15, bathrooms, sqft_lot, lat.

Figure 11: Variable importance chart (Random Forest)

5. Feature Selection (Wrapper Method)

Using a wrapper method (backward elimination), we selected 8 variables that maintain high classification performance. With these 8 features, RandomForest achieves 82.2% accuracy — only 1.6 percentage points less than using all variables.

Figure 12–13: Feature selection results and performance comparison

6. Conclusions

The best configuration is the OrdinalClassClassifier with RandomForest (60 trees) applied to 5 ordinal classes, achieving an accuracy of 83.8% and Cohen's Kappa of 0.715.

The most influential variables for predicting housing quality are: square footage of the living area, number of bathrooms, lot size, latitude, and neighbourhood quality indicators (sqft_living15, sqft_lot15).
