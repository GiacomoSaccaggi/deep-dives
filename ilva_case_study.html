The ILVA Case: Health vs Work

In-depth Articles

1. Introduction and Context

Taranto is a city located on the Ionian Sea in southern Italy, covering an area of 209.64 km². Its strategic position has granted it historical importance since the Roman Empire, when it served as a key port and commercial hub. Through the centuries, Taranto maintained its relevance as a military base for the Italian Navy.

The city's modern demographic trajectory was profoundly shaped by the steel industry. The construction of the IV Centro Siderurgico (Fourth Steelmaking Center) transformed Taranto from a mid-sized southern Italian city into an industrial powerhouse. On October 10, 1965, President Giuseppe Saragat officially inaugurated what was then Europe's largest steel production center, marking the apex of Italy's post-war industrial policy in the Mezzogiorno.

This decision, rooted in the logic of state-driven industrialization, would define Taranto's economic identity for decades — but also set the stage for one of Italy's most significant conflicts between the right to work and the right to health.



2. History of ILVA

ILVA was founded on February 1, 1905 in Genoa, with an initial capital of 12 million lire. The company took its name from the Latin name for the island of Elba, historically associated with iron mining.

Key milestones in ILVA's corporate history:

  • 1911 — Formation of a consortium among major Italian steel producers
  • 1917 — Merger into "Ilva Alti Forni e Acciaierie d'Italia," consolidating Italian steelmaking capacity during World War I
  • Post-1929 — The Great Depression devastated the Italian steel sector. The state intervened through IRI (Istituto per la Ricostruzione Industriale), which acquired ILVA's controlling shares
  • 1930s–1940s — Oscar Sinigaglia developed his rationalization plan for Italian steelmaking, advocating for integrated-cycle plants located near ports (to minimize raw material transport costs)
  • 1940–1945 — WWII caused extensive damage to Italian industrial infrastructure
  • 1949 — Reconstruction began under the Sinigaglia Plan, with Taranto selected as the ideal site for a new coastal integrated steelworks
  • 1965 — The Taranto plant becomes operational under the Italsider brand (IRI's steel subsidiary), inaugurated by President Saragat


3. Privatization (1988–1995)

The wave of privatizations that swept through Italian state industry in the late 1980s and 1990s reached the steel sector in stages:

  • 1988 — The Cornigliano (Genoa) plant was sold to the Riva Group, marking the first major divestiture
  • 1989 — Finsider (IRI's steel holding company) entered liquidation, signaling the end of state-owned steelmaking in Italy
  • 1995 — Emilio Riva won the competitive tender for the Taranto plant, paying 1,649 billion lire. The Riva Group's bid prevailed over competing offers from Lucchini and the Franco-Belgian group Usinor

Under private ownership, the Taranto plant — now operating under the ILVA brand — became the largest integrated steelworks in Europe, with production capacity exceeding 10 million tonnes per year. However, questions about environmental compliance and worker health, already present during the state-ownership era, intensified under private management focused on maximizing output.



4. The Judicial Process

On September 7, 2013, GIP (Giudice per le Indagini Preliminari) Patrizia Lodisco of the Taranto Tribunal issued a landmark preventive seizure order targeting 8.1 billion euros — the estimated profit derived from environmental crimes committed by ILVA's management.

The judicial motivations rested on two pillars:

  • Environmental damage — systematic contamination of air, soil, and water in the Taranto area, with documented exceedances of regulatory emission limits
  • Health risks — exposure of workers and the surrounding population to hazardous substances (including dioxins, benzo[a]pyrene, and heavy metals) through inadequate equipment, insufficient maintenance, and failure to implement available pollution-control technologies

The seizure order represented an unprecedented judicial intervention in Italian industrial history, effectively threatening the operational continuity of the country's largest private employer in the steel sector.



5. Legislative Response

Faced with the potential shutdown of ILVA and the loss of approximately 12,000 direct jobs (plus an estimated 8,000 in the supply chain), the Italian government intervened with Decree-Law 207/2012, issued by the Monti Government.

The decree established a novel legal framework:

  • Strategic national interest — ILVA was designated as a plant of strategic national interest, a classification that triggered special legal protections
  • Seizure annulment — The preventive seizure was effectively neutralized, conditional on compliance with a mandatory environmental remediation plan
  • Environmental plan (AIA) — The Autorizzazione Integrata Ambientale (Integrated Environmental Authorization) established binding emission reduction targets, subject to ARPA (Regional Environmental Protection Agency) monitoring and approval
  • 5-year compliance timeline — The plant was granted five years to achieve full compliance with environmental standards, allowing continued operation during the transition

This legislative intervention raised immediate constitutional questions about the separation of powers and the hierarchy of fundamental rights.



6. Constitutional Court — Sentence 85/2013

The Constitutional Court's ruling in Sentence 85/2013 addressed the fundamental tension at the heart of the ILVA case: can the legislature override a judicial seizure in the name of economic and employment interests?

The Court examined the balance among three constitutional rights:

  • Article 4 — The right to work ("The Republic recognizes the right of all citizens to work")
  • Article 32 — The right to health ("The Republic safeguards health as a fundamental right of the individual and as a collective interest")
  • Article 41 — Freedom of private enterprise ("Private economic enterprise is free. It cannot be conducted in conflict with social utility or in a manner that could harm safety, liberty, and human dignity")

The Ruling

The Court upheld the constitutionality of Decree-Law 207/2012, articulating the doctrine of systemic protection:

"All fundamental rights protected by the Constitution are in a relationship of reciprocal integration, and it is not possible to identify one that has absolute prevalence over the others. The protection must always be systemic and not fractured into a series of uncoordinated and potentially conflicting norms."

This principle established that:

  • No single constitutional right — including health — can claim absolute priority in all circumstances
  • The legislature has legitimate authority to balance competing constitutional values
  • Such balancing must be reasonable and proportionate, ensuring that no right is entirely sacrificed
  • The conditional nature of the continued operation (mandatory environmental plan, timeline, monitoring) represented an adequate safeguard


7. Court of Cassation — December 20, 2013

On December 20, 2013, the Court of Cassation (Italy's highest court of appeal) ruled on the specific matter of the preventive seizure, invalidating it. The Court's decision aligned with the legislative and constitutional framework established by Decree-Law 207/2012 and Sentence 85/2013, confirming that the seizure could not stand given the government's intervention and the mandatory environmental compliance plan.

This decision closed the immediate judicial threat to ILVA's operations, shifting the focus from punitive measures to the implementation of the re-engineering plan.

The production cap of 8 million tonnes per year — imposed at zero direct cost but with significant revenue implications — represented the regulatory counterpart to the infrastructure investments.



9. Conclusions

The ILVA case represents a paradigmatic example of the tension between industrial development and environmental/health protection in contemporary democracies. The Italian Constitutional Court's doctrine of systemic protection — rejecting the absolute prevalence of any single fundamental right — offers a conceptual framework for resolving similar conflicts.

Key takeaways from the ILVA precedent:

  • Constitutional balancing — The right to health cannot automatically override the right to work, nor vice versa. Resolution requires proportionate measures that advance all competing interests
  • Conditional operation — Allowing continued industrial activity subject to binding environmental compliance plans represents a middle path between immediate shutdown and unconstrained operation
  • Temporal dimension — The 5-year compliance timeline acknowledges that environmental remediation of heavy industry requires substantial capital investment and engineering time
  • Institutional cooperation — The resolution required coordination among the judiciary, legislature, executive, and specialized technical agencies (ARPA)

The ILVA case continues to serve as a reference model for industrial-environmental conflicts in EU member states, demonstrating both the possibilities and limitations of constitutional balancing in the face of irreversible environmental and health damage.
